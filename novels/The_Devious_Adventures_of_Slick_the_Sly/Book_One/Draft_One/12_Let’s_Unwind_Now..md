The first order of business was a nice, long, steaming hot bath. He slipped out of his lair, still in his villainous activities outfit sans the badly damaged gear. After he started the water flowing, Slick took a long moment to examine himself in the mirror. Oh, what a disaster he was. His black shirt, though mostly in tact where the jetpack sat, was torn to shreds. His sleeves were shredded beyond repair, his arms had many a cut and were practically one large bruise. Meanwhile, his slacks weren’t much better. He peeled his shirt from his back and examined the damage. He had glass cuts at the base of his neck and the small of his back. In short, he hurt everywhere.

His face, however, didn’t look too bad. The helmet did its job admirably. Once the batch was full and all bubbly with wonderful goodness, Slick stripped and stepped in. He laid there for a very long time, and in fact he have fallen asleep for a while. The bubbles soothed and relaxed his sore muscles, and his tub wouldn’t let the water get cold. In this moment, that was one of his absolute greatest inventions.

Sometime after he dozed off, his doorbell rang. Groggy and upset, Slick slipped out of his tub’s warm embrace and did a quick dry on himself. He pulled his fluffy black robe on and cinched it shut as he strolled to the stairs. At first his steps were difficult and painful, but as he stretched his muscles they felt a bit more natural. He wasn’t all fine and dandy, of course, but the bath helped.

Whoever rang the doorbell grew impatient. Slick heard a steady knock on the stout wood as he reached the bottom of the stairs. He strode to the monitor and flicked it on to see who would disturb him at the ungodly hour of--eight fifteen. “Huh,” Slick grunted as as looked from his watch to the monitor. He thought it must be later than that.

Outside his door stood Greg, and Slick couldn’t for the life of him deduce why his neighbor would be here. The villain let Greg stand on the porch for several long moments before he opened the door with an exhausted sigh. It hurt to move. “Yes?” Slick asked as he arched a brow at his neighbor.

“What a night,” Greg exhaled. “Nevermoore paid me a visit. Not entirely sure why.” Slick wondered about that as well. “Sorry if the ruckus disturbed you.”

“Oh, I hardly noticed,” Slick muttered as he poked his head out the door to cast an inquiring eyebrow at Greg’s house.

“Really?” Greg asked, incredulous at Slick’s response. “It was a nightmare, and so loud. My house is in ruins. Well, the first floor is.” Slick had to admit the man was right. The entire front wall from the door to the corner of the house was destroyed. Glass, wood, wallpaper, flooring, and anything else Slick could imagine littered the lawn. He couldn’t see inside from this angle, but he’d been over there. Greg’s home was a disaster. Slick was somewhat responsible for that, it’s true, but still. That Nevermoore was a nuisance, devoid of finesse and tact. “I might have heard something. The T.V. was on,” Slick tossed out, distracted by his examination of the ruined home.

“Right,” Greg said after an uncomfortable pause, “well, why I came over was to ask a favor. I can’t sleep here, so I’m going to check into a hotel. I was wondering if you could just keep half an eye on the house. Make sure nothing too untowards happens while I’m away.”

Slick grunted, and it was only when Greg stared at him for a long, awkward moment that Slick realized his response did not relate an adequate amount of information. “Yes, um, why don’t you stay here?” For all the world, Slick couldn’t begin to fathom why he extended the invitation. Greg blinked in surprise. Slick blinked back.

“I mean, that’s kind of you, but that’s not necessary.” There it was, Slick’s out. Gracious ole’ Greg had lined it up perfectly. He just had to say the right words.

“It’s really no imposition. It’s getting late, I’m sure you don’t want to drive to a hotel. Besides, I have five guest bedrooms I never use.” What was he doing!?! Those were not the words. Slick chocked it up to extreme exhaustion.

Greg scrunched his mouth up as he considered the offer. “Well, if you’re sure, that’d be nice I suppose.”

At this point, Slick had no choice. He’d gone too far. “Of course. Come in, come in.” He stepped aside to allow Greg entrance. The man dropped his bag by the stairs as Slick shut the door.

Now that Greg was inside, all avenues of conversation fizzled out and the two men stood in silence. For just a moment it was companionable, but now the awkwardness had reached such epic levels that even Slick could sense it. Neither seemed capable of breaking their strange bondage, so Slick shook his brain awake and grasped desperately for any topic to fill the silence. “Are you hungry?”

Greg jolted at the question. “What? Oh, yeah. I haven’t eaten since, goodness, I have no idea.” Slick nodded then gestured towards the kitchen.

“Let’s see what we have,” he muttered as he went to the refrigerator and pulled the double doors wide open. If the world were to end, if grocery stores were bombed and pillaged and no food could be procured, Slick would be unprepared indeed. Inside the fridge was a half empty and somewhat rotten gallon of milk, three quarters of an apple, a peanut butter and jelly sandwich that Slick had not at all cared for, and something that appeared to have evolved in the vegetable drawer. Greg began to rummage through cabinets with little success.

When all possible hiding places had been searched, the two men resigned themselves to the fact that there was no food in the house. They gazed at each other again until Slick said, “take out?”

“Reading my mind, brother,” Greg replied with a chuckle. For the next three minutes and forty-seven seconds the two had a blast as they poured over the menu and called the restaurant. Once the food was on its way, they settled back into their silence. Normally, Greg might be more talkative, but Slick could tell the man was tired. He’d had a heck of a night, though it was nothing compared to Slick’s. In the end, they leaned against the counter and and stared off into space. It took three tries at the doorbell for Slick to hear the delivery driver and move to accept their food.

While Slick signed the credit slip, Greg grabbed the brown bags of food and carried them to the dining table. Slick joined him as soon as he shut the door. They settled at either end of the large table and dug into their meal. As Slick slid the saucy noodles onto his fork, he took a moment to adjust his position in the chair. This was mighty uncomfortable. With a quick glance around, he realized that this room was very unfamiliar to him. Surely he’d eaten here before. He had to have eaten in the dining room before. Slick dropped his fork and examined the room about him, and it became clear that he had never seen it before. He wasn’t even sure where the artwork had come from. Beyond that, he’d never really looked at the table.

Greg seemed uncomfortable as well. “You want to take this in the media room?” With a sigh, Greg said, “Yes. I just want to melt into a couch right now.” Slick nodded then scooped up his containers of food and shuffled off to their next destination.

The media room was arranged in an interior room, so there were no windows to interrupt the view on the ninety inch television mounted to the far wall. Arrayed before that icon of digital entertainment were lounge chairs the likes of which most men could always dream of. I would try to describe them, but there is no way to adequately capture their splendid comfort and style without first seeing then sitting in one. On top of this, Slick had installed the best of sounds systems. This was a true man made marvel, and Greg understood that the moment Slick nudged the switch to toggle on the recessed lights set around the room.

“Holy,” Greg began. “I thought I had a good set up. How’s the image on that beast? It must be phenomenal.”

Slick shrugged. “I wouldn’t know. This is the first time I’ve used it.” Greg stared at Slick like he’d just admitted to getting his kicks by hurling puppies at babies or something equally abhorrent.

“You’re a strange man, Mik.” he said at last.

Slick shrugged that off and settled into his chair while Greg took the one next to him. “I take my entertainment elsewhere.” Disinterested as he seemed, Slick had to admit that when he powered up the system, it was a near religious experience. I’d use other words, but we’re trying to keep this tale family friendly. Let’s just say, even Slick was moved by the splendor of such a large screen in so unbelievably high a definition.

The two settled on a movie that Slick didn’t really remember afterwards. Something with copious amounts of explosions and a fair number of seductive women. Slick and Greg ate their dinner. They shouted, laughed, and cheered together. The movie was so good, they put on another just like it. Never in his life had Slick experienced such a thing. He’d never really had a friend. Reginald perched up on the coffee table, as if to dispute that thought, but Slick didn’t mind. Tired as we was, it was nice to take a break from being him.

So tired were they that neither man made it through the second movie. For hours they passed out in the recliners, until Greg pulled himself up and roused Slick. As a good host should, Slick got Greg situated in a guest room then took himself to bed. He slept very, very well that night.

In the morning, Slick extended an open ended invitation to Greg, which he gladly accepted. Greg had contractors in that very next day, so he spent most of his day over at his place anyhow, so it was no great inconvenience to Slick. Still battered from his fight with Nevermoore, Slick called out of work for the next week. With the feathered felon behind bars once more, the city was quiet, and since Greg’s house was in shambles, Slick had little to do in that department. As such, he put his villainous exploits on hold. There’d be time enough for that

later.

At last the day came when Slick had to return to work. He didn’t want to, but if he didn’t maintain that cover, he’d lose his only real connection to Fiona. Slick sighed at the thought of her name. It was the first time she’d entered his mind in a week, and it felt weird. Something was clearly wrong with Slick. He’d have to step up his plans to win her affections. Despite the resolution, however, Slick couldn’t bring himself to consider it while he drove to the Book Shelf. Instead, he cranked up some show tunes and rolled the windows down. The brisk autumn air was a relief as it washed over him.

When he pulled into work, Slick’s hair was a mess. He didn’t care. Who was he trying to impress? Our villain shuffled across the parking lot and into the building. Chuck gave him a nice, long lecture about how long he was out, but Slick didn’t hear any of it. His mind was at rest. No doubt he’d get it going again before much longer, but for now he quite enjoyed the serenity of the past week.

Chuck finished his tirade, somewhat deflated without a response from Slick, and shuffled on his way. Slick meandered back to the young adult section and began to work books to the shelf. He heard her laugh before he saw her. Fiona strolled into work and ignited Slick’s flesh.

Suddenly, he wished he was back in his car in that cool autumn morning.

Slowly, the cogs began to turn once more in his head. The inkling of a new venture came to mind. This time, it couldn’t fail. Fiona would be his. She would love him, and everything would be all hunky dory--my words, not his. It was rough around the edges, and he still needed to knock the rust and cobwebs from his brain, but there was potential. He could see himself the hero, the victor that won the hand of the ever elusive Fiona. She would stroll up to him with that graceful, enchanting walk of hers. He could see the smile on her lips, perfect as the marble statues of the great masters. Okay, that was a bit weird, but still she had nice lips. On that day, she’d look right at him. Finally, she’d see him. She would see the real him, like he always knew she would, and in that day--

Oh, wait, no, this was happening. This wasn’t a daydream, she approached Slick this very day. There were no plans, no machinations in place to win her heart. He wasn’t at all prepared. What did they call this, “winging it?” Slick did not at all like this. Fiona, beautiful, dear Fiona, stopped just a few steps from Slick. He froze right in the middle of placing a book on the shelf. His body wouldn’t react. He couldn’t move. Why couldn’t he move! Panic set in as Slick looked for a way out, any way out. The problem was, he couldn’t take his eyes of Fiona and that greatly complicated his search for an escape.

“Hi, Mik. How are you feeling?” She spoke. Oh no, she spoke, what was he supposed to do? He wasn’t prepared for this. No one had prepared him for this. Slick’s heart beat so fast it felt like it would explode. He wished it would.

Alright Slick, bring it in. This was his chance. Was it a perfect meeting? Of course not, but so few things in life were. He could still save this. First, he had to speak. Just speak, but not just anything. Make it something cool, sophisticated, and smooth. He’d read plenty of books on this subject, as well as various websites of varying degrees of credibility, and he knew confidence was key. If she knew what he was like inside, she’d run away screaming, which ironically is exactly what Slick wanted to do. He couldn’t, though, because this was it. This was his moment in the sun.

The silence stretched on, and each second only added to Slick’s distress. Fiona’s genuine smile lasted quite some time, but Slick saw it was starting to fade to a weak, pitying sort of thing. He couldn’t allow that. His tongue must be loosed. Alright now, remember, something cool. Something collected. Confidence was key. He should tell her that one joke he knows. It’s a real kicker. In an instant, Slick pieced together a very compelling, suave sort of conversation. He didn’t know where he drew from, but suddenly, he knew what to say. At this moment, he knew he had the right answer. He said, “Uh-uh-h-h,” and then he gulped before continuing with, “Um, uh-h-uhi-Hi.”

Really? You’ve got to be kidding me.
