Thus far, he’d made use of primarily high frequency waves, but now he felt his bones rumble as the whomp whomp of the bass waves blasted out over the fire. If he was going to make a habit of this, he’d need some earplugs.

Still, despite all that, it seemed to be working. The flames receded as he readjusted the direction of his emitters to ensure even distribution. At last, the fire died. Slick gestured for the woman to go, and she dashed through the smoke and over the charred carpet. Next she was down the stairs and out of Slick’s life. Good riddance.

Slick stepped to the edge of the broken window and viewed the mayhem below. Smoke billowed from the helicopter, but it still seemed to be operational for the moment. Slick tried to track where each individual player was, but the fighting was too interconnected now to be so easily traced. While no one else had joined so far, the bedlam had attracted a great deal of onlookers. The news crews were out in full force, while cell phones filmed every second of the battle. Cop cars blockaded the street, but the police officers didn’t know what to do.

Even the superpowered forces of the city took interest in the night’s events. A dark flicker drew Slick’s gaze to the top of a nearby building. Shades leaned against a support beam and watched with his dark, placid gaze. Those sunglasses he always sported only served to heighten the unreadability of his expression. Even Slick couldn’t gauge what went on in that man’s head. For now, Shades seemed content to just observe. What would happen if he choose to participate? His power was not the strongest one on the field, but he could use it to devastating results. Still, Slick doubted he’d get to see. A thief by trade, Shades didn’t often get involved with others.

Slick took a moment more to look over the street when his gaze settled on something he found quite interesting. It was the same man from the bank, when Slick investigated Major Optimism’s robbery. The gentleman just stood there, hands in his hoodie pocket as he watched the scene unfold around him. After a moment, he looked up to the window where Slick stood, as if he knew who was behind that odd mask. The experience unnerved Slick, and few things could do that. Since nothing of value could be gleaned from this observation, he shook of the heebie jeebies and brought his mind back around to the battle.

This was madness. Utter ridiculousness. While this had all started well enough, now there was no purpose to any of it. Wanton destruction of property, terror in the streets, none of it served any point. Sure, Slick was a villain, but he had to have a purpose. All actions had consequences, whether right or wrong, and the world was only hurt by the chaos Slick now gazed at. Even the heros were oblivious to it all. It seemed that it fell to Slick to fix things.

He cast another look around the environment and gauged the factors that most needed his attention. The list narrowed to three items; Megaton, Nevermoore, and the helicopter. First thing first, Slick had to stop that police chopper from shooting up the whole neighborhood. He was sure they met well, but that was just ridiculously unsafe, not to mention the expense they incurred with every shot. The police force might be largely privatized, but they still got a cut of the taxes paid by the citizens of Main City.

It was time to get to work. Slick blasted from the building and bore down on the helicopter. He’d been so long out of the fight, the police seemed to have forgotten about him, which made his job a great deal simpler. About halfway there, Megaton once more collided with Slick. This time, the villain grabbed him by the jetpack and started to ride him down to the ground.

“Where you been, helmet head? Which side you on anyways?” Megaton was not a mental giant, and it showed in his simplistic speech. “Eh, no biggie. I’ma pummel you anyways.”

Right, first priority shifted. The helicopter could wait. Slick had to avoid getting punched. He tried to struggle free, but Megaton’s grip was too strong. The villain laughed like a maniac as the concrete grew far closer than Slick was comfortable with. Megaton would survive, Slick likely would not. Oh, and now Megaton pulled his fist back to deliver his promised pounding. Just great.

Slick was out of options. He wanted to hold off on using this bit of tech, because its current form was a prototype, and would have consequences that would affect him the rest of the fight. Only in the case of an emergency did he decide to give it a try. Forget it, this was about as an emergency as Slick was ever to have. He balled his fist and activated his Stun Glove, the same one from the museum heist. It took all of one second to charge. Thankfully, Megaton was having too much fun building the anticipation. Slick drove his fist into the brute’s gut and the shock sent the red-caped villain flying off of Slick.

Free from his assailant, Slick powered up his jetpack and slowed his fall until he landed gently on top of a car. His right gauntlet popped, fizzled, sparked, then burst into flame. Slick waved his hand around, but that only seemed to fan the flames. In a panic, he got the glove off and tossed the thing onto the ground where it could do little harm to his person. It only took a few moments for the fire to burn out, but it reached truly epic proportions before it did.

He was afraid of that. It took too much juice to use the Stun Glove, and when he did it shorted everything already going on in the gauntlet. He could solve that little issue, of course, but he needed the time. That meant that they had one use in them, and then Slick’s primary weapon and defense was useless. Even if it hadn’t burst into flame, Slick knew the electronics would be shot. The sonic emitters wouldn’t work. Worse, he lost throttle control with his right glove. Thankfully, he brought back up.

Before he worried about that, Slick jumped from the car and reached down to pick up his discarded gauntlet. It was still quite hot, so Slick reached for the edge of his cape to wrap around it. It was at this point that it dawned on him that he had worn a cape over a jetpack. Most of the fabric had burned away, and in retrospect that explained the burning aroma he’d sensed most of the evening. He still had a bit of cape left, so he pulled the thing off and wrapped it around the smoking glove. With a great deal of care he secured it to his belt and that was that. Once he was satisfied with its placement, he pulled the original throttle control from his pouch and linked it up with the jetpack. It wouldn’t be quite as responsive as the glove had been, but it would do. He’d have to be careful about punching most of these guys, though. They could take a beating, and his fist could not. Pity his right was his dominant hand.

Now that all that was settled, Slick looked around for any sign of Megaton. The beast of a man lay unconscious down the street from him, not too far from the police blockade. Slick strode over and nudged the supervillain with his foot. There was no responses, though the man’s chest still rose so at least Slick hadn’t killed him.

“Hey, handcuff this man!” Slick called to the police officers.

One in particular seemed to think he was the focus of Slick’s demand. He shook his head and stuttered, “Not hardly. I’ve seen the punches he’s thrown out. What’s some handcuffs to do?”

Slick sighed in exasperation. Amateurs. “Are you kidding me? Don’t you know anything about the villains in this city? Megaton doesn’t possess super strength. His power requires the movement and build up of a punch, which he can’t exactly do in handcuffs, now can he?” The cop reluctantly shook his head. “Good, now secure him. I’ve other things to do tonight.” He didn’t wait to see if his demand was met, but he assumed it would be. If nothing else, the officer would be a hero, and who can resist that temptation?

Once more Slick gunned the throttle and shot towards the helicopter. He was starting to get used to this whole jetpack thing. Sure, the manual stick was a touch less accurate, but thankfully it was the throttle and not the directional control. That’s the main reason he chose to give up his right hand, not his left. Maybe now he could actually deal with this police chopper. How he hadn’t been shot yet was a miracle, but Slick was sure that those officers had caused more injuries than any of the heroes and villains. This was excluding Heatburst, of course. His fire was everywhere. At least the firefighters earned their pay tonight.

To approach the helicopter from the sides was near to suicide, as plenty of the gathered individuals could attest. Slick now had a clear angle at the belly of the beast, and so help him if one more thing interrupted his attack on this aircraft, Slick wouldn’t be responsible for what came next. As it turned out, he made it to the underbelly of the chopper without interruption, so the world could rest easy knowing the wrath of Slick the Sly was focused on other, smaller targets.

A very important question now needed to be answered. How was Slick going to deal with this machine? He was down to one sonic gauntlet, and he wasn’t sure that he could do much damage to the chopper anyways. He wouldn’t have to, come to think of it, if he could just breach the fuel tank. Yes, he lacked the means to do so, but he could come up with something he was sure. Maybe if he drew Heatburst’s attention, he could get a few fireballs thrown his way. Come to think of it, he could find a sturdy enough piece of debris in the street to get through the metal.

It was doubtful, not impossible.

Wait a minute. Was that an access hatch? It seemed silly to put one there, right behind the fuel tank. Granted, that area of the helicopter also housed the transmission and the like, so maintenance would be required. Slick inched over to the access panel and prayed the chopper pilots continued to stay as stationary as they had been. Slick managed to get around to the panel, but was dismayed to find he had no ready means of opening it. He took another moment to look around at his options.

It would be wonderful to destroy the rotors, but Slick had no means on hand to accomplish such a task. His next idea involved the windshield. It seemed the safest avenue of attack, so he might as well. As he hovered there, he dug around the pouches on his belt. He didn’t have much, but he did think he saw… ah yes, there it was. Given that he’d be flying around skyscrapers, he decided a glass suction handle would be a wise choice. It took up too much room in his pouch, of course, but it would prove useful if he had to, say, grab ahold of something while sliding down the glass of a hundred story building. It should provide a hand-hold on a helicopter windshield just as well.

Apprehensive though he was, Slick inched his way along beneath the chopper. Once he reached the nose, it was now or never. With a deep gulp he blasted out in front then on top of the windshield. The pilot shouted something I shall not repeat and turned to his comrades.

“Bogey on the windshield!” the pilot exclaimed. Slick locked down the suction handle, tucked the throttle controller into his belt, and held on for dear life.

“Shoot him, man,” someone else called.

“You crazy? We’ll bring the whole thing down!” the pilot screamed.

“What do you think he’s tryin’ for?” another voice chimed in.

Slick placed his left hand flat against the cockpit. He had no idea if this would work, but

he was willing to try it.

“What’s he doin, yo?” the fourth officer asked.

“No idea,” the pilot shouted.

Slick curled his fingers and felt the glass vibrate as the sonic waves spread through the glass. Apparently it was audible inside the cockpit too.

“What the heck’s he doing, man! Make it stop!” All gunfire stopped as the officers covered their ears. Slick raised the frequency slowly in search of just the right pitch. The glass bucked more and more under his grasp.

“Shake ‘im off!” It wasn’t a bad idea, and the pilot rushed to oblige. Slick held on tight as the chopper cut down and swerved to the right. From there, the pilot bobbed and weaved in the air in a wild attempt to shake Slick loose. Somehow, our villain still held on. It was a tad more difficult to keep the sonic gauntlet in place though.

Slick saw it in the reflection in the windshield before the chopper hit the building. Its rotors tore into the glass like paper mache until they hit one of the steel support beams. From there, it got so very ugly so very fast. Somehow, the pilot got the chopper away from the structure, but it was too late. Half the rotor was gone and the other was horribly bent. Slick knew a sinking ship when he saw one, and he let go of the handle to grab the throttle control from his

belt.

At that moment the tail rotor spun into the building, and the whole helicopter shook from the impact. Slick slid from the nose of the chopper and bounced against the chassis on his way down. Worst of all, he lost his grip on the throttle remote and it sailed from his grasp. He fumbled for it, and even got a finger on the blasted thing, but it was to no avail. The remote was gone. This proved problematic because Slick’s jetpack was not on. He could maybe formulate a way to increase the throttle on the pack itself, but as he tumbled towards the street it became painfully obvious that he wouldn’t have the time. This very well could be it, the last caper Slick ever pulled.

Hope was dim until he caught sight of Nevermoore’s wings below him. The villain had his arms around Teledine and seemed oblivious to all else. If Slick could only shift just a bit more, he’d be right no top of the villain. He tried to swim or struggle to through the air, but he had no idea if it was working. The distance closed, one meter at a time. That time was going by way too fast for Slick’s comfort. Soon it would be too late, but things appeared to be going his way. He was almost there and…

He had it! Slick collided with Nevermoore, and the startled villain dropped Teledine as he and Slick plummeted towards the street. Teledine, now aware of the plight of the helicopter, was able to grab hold of it and lower it slowly to the ground to prevent any further damage and loss of life. Slick was not concerned with the helicopter. His mind was too focused on the impending splat of the concrete. He’d felt this far too many times this night. He hoped this would be the last time for a long, long while.

Nevermoore struggled to get his wings working. Now that he was close, Slick could see the damage the villain had taken throughout the course of the night. How he was still fighting, Slick didn’t know, but he better keep it up for a few more minutes. Slick had a death grip on Nevermoore’s waist, and he wasn’t about to let go any time soon. Finally, the bird brain got his wings working and with five great flaps, their speed slowed drastically. Content that he was no longer going fast enough to die on impact, Slick made his move.

With a great deal of effort, Slick reached up and grabbed hold of the harness Nevermoore wore in lieu of a shirt. The villain squawked in protest as Slick pulled himself up level with the strong, broad wings.

“What are you doing?” Nevermoore roared. “You’ll kill us both!” Slick grunted from the strain of his task, but otherwise gave no answer as he wrapped his legs around Nevermoore’s stomach.

It was difficult to not feel as though the wings tried to beat him to death in his current position. He planned to solve that soon, though. Once he felt secure in his place on Nevermoore’s back, Slick released his hold on the harness and wrapped one arm around the bottom of each wing. He grabbed hold of the stout bones at the top of the wings and pulled with all of his might. Nevermoore tried to fight Slick off, but the villain was too weak from his other efforts of the night. With Slick’s added weight and hold on his wings, the pair began to fall again.

Slick released his legs from Nevermoore’s torso and slid his knees into the birdman’s lower back. With a great deal of effort, Slick pushed Nevermoore into a horizontal position. Now, he rode the man down like a kneeboard, if a kneeboard had two giant wings for handholds. Seeing the proximity of the ground, Nevermoore freaked and used all his strength to try and wrench his wings free. Slick was too close now to risk being toppled or worse, taken further up into the air. The night had been too long, and it need to end.

Nevermoore gave a screech of triumph when Slick pulled his left hand away from the wing. The appendage flapped like mad, and it took a great deal of effort for Slick to stay upright. This had to end, and now. He placed his left palm against Nevermoore’s right wing and began to close his fist. The sonic emitter blasted out sound waves, and Slick intensified the projection until he at last heard the cracking of bone.

The feathered felon screamed in pain, a terrible noise part man and part bird. He didn’t have long to wait, for another fifteen seconds and he slammed into the pavement with Slick on top. Though he tried to roll off the birdman, Slick predominantly failed and flew out into the street. It was not pleasant. The pair had been low enough that no serious injury occured, but Slick would feel the wounds from the impact for a good little while. Once his eyes could focus again, Slick looked over to see Nevermoore still breathing, which was a victory. The police already rushed forward to arrest the feathered menace, and Greg would be safe for a good long while now.

In the commotion, Slick was mostly forgotten. Champion was too busy with the reporters to notice Slick stand shakily. He shook his head to clear it, then took stock of his surroundings. The street was a disaster, but like Slick thought the entire scene died clear away the moment he finished off Nevermoore. Now emergency teams sifted through the rubble and put out fires.

Heatburst sat off to the side with a cigarette, content with the mayhem he’d caused that day.

Slick didn’t much care for him.

Teledine, of course, ended up in front of the cameras with Champion. The only difference is, once Champion finished his statement he left the reporters and went to ask the police sergeant what he could do to help. Teledine remained glued to the cameras. It was just as well.

Slick couldn’t deal with him anyways.

Twenty feet away on the asphalt sat his throttle control. Slick tilted his head in confusion. He built sturdy devices and all, but that thing should have been smashed. How did it survive the fall? He gingerly stepped over and stooped to pick it up. Every inch of his body ached. Why had he done this? Was it worth all this pain and suffering? Now that his adrenaline began to fade, Slick just wanted a nice, long bath and to go to bed. He wondered if the remote still worked. With a twitch of his finger he fired up the jetpack with an approving nod. He had no idea how that happened, but he was too tired to care. He’d take it.

Annoyingly, the noise of the jetpack drew the attention of the reporters. Each one relished the chance to be the one to talk to the newest hero in town, and the whole flock of them stormed over to Slick as he stood there, not entirely sure what was happening. Before he could even try, they peppered him with so many questions, he wanted to just blast them all with his sonic emitter. He didn’t, not because it would be wrong, but because he was far too tired.

“Where are you from?”

“What made you be a hero?”

“What’s the deal with that suit? Are those supposed to look so raggedy?”

“Yeah, what is the costume concept? I’m at a loss.”

“Why’d you choose to fight Nevermoore your first time out?”

“Is this your first time out?”

“What’s your name?”

That last question silenced the entire press corps. It seemed that they all wanted to know the answer to it, and Slick could see that several of them kicked themselves for not asking it first. Champion stopped his conversation and paced over to watch the proceedings with a keen note of interest. He respected Slick’s time in the spotlight, though, and kept his distance.

Teledine did as well, but for a completely different reason. Disappointed that all the cameras

now focused on someone else, Teledine began to sulk and flew away with his shoulders low and his head hung.

Slick pondered if he should answer. Did he owe these jackals anything? What use would they have with his name anyways? Would he give his real villain name? He hadn’t really considered it, if he were to be honest. He looked out at the expectant cameras as he thought. Was now the time to make himself public? It didn’t much matter anymore. There were pictures and videos of him everywhere now. No doubt he was all over Palface and Metoob. He may as well give them what they sought.

“Slick the Sly,” he rasped. The reporters exploded in a flurry of enthusiasm. The drastic difference between the dead quiet of a moment ago and the cacophony of noise that now assaulted Slick was astonishing. They chittered to each other, begged for exclusive interviews, peppered him with personal questions, and one even asked him out. There wasn’t much sense to be made from the madness in front of him until one question in particular stuck out to him.

“What kind of hero do you see yourself as?”

What kind indeed? Slick laughed. Between the exhaustion, smoke fumes, the mask over his mouth, and his overall exhaustion, there was something unsettling about the laughter that issued forth from him. It disturbed the reporters enough that they quieted down and looked at each other in confusion and apprehension.

“Oh, you fools. I am no hero.” Before they could hound him for insight into what that meant, or ask for photos or whatever else they had in mind, Slick kicked his pack into full gear and blasted off into the night.

From that point, it was a leisurely trip home. There were no giant ravens, or flying neanderthals out to kill him. His nemesis was safe from the danger imposed by other, lesser villains. Now that he was public though, he’d have to work on his gear. It barely saw him through the night. He would need to be better equipped for the future. Then there was the issue of the shorting problem with the integrated Stun Gloves. Bah, these were issues for another day. Now, he needed to rest. His mind drifted off on it’s own as he mechanically steered towards home. He didn’t dare take the yard home, not after the visibility he’d had tonight. Instead, he found one of those pesky sewer entrances and tucked in there. He was so tired, he didn’t even see the drone that trailed him until he disappeared beneath the city.
