It proved easy enough to leave the Peterson Center undetected, which only served to increase Slick’s apprehension. Once he was safely ensconced in the bushes again he pulled out his tablet and reengaged all the security systems he’d disabled. He even took the extra time to hack into the radios the guards used and patch it through to his own specialized bluetooth headset. The chatter at the moment was mundane and dull, but that was a good sign. Now that these additional preparations were finished, Slick tucked the tablet away and started off towards the Main Gallery.

The painting proved to be an awkward load to carry, even if it was one of the smaller pieces in the museum. He trotted up the steps to the patio and tucked himself behind a pillar to prepare for the next phase. No camera could see him here, but such blind spots were rare inside the building. He’d need a similar tactic to escape detection here as he used in the Peterson Center. Unfortunately, the two buildings were on closed security systems, so he’d need to deal with the Main Gallery on its own.

To his eternal pleasure, Slick found that the security systems here were much older and far less sophisticated than in the brand new Peterson Center. It took him no time at all to hack in and get a loop set into place. It wasn’t as sophisticated as what he’d implemented at the last building, but then again the security team here wasn’t even close to the level employed at the newest addition to the Main City Art Museum. In fact, Gus had been employed here since the museum first opened, and since he was too old to go on patrol he usually got placed in front of the monitors. Most nights, he passed out about an hour ago. Sure enough, there was Guss on the camera snoozing away as always.

Slick smiled as he tucked the tablet away and slipped into the gallery. While emergency lights were spread throughout the gallery for safety reasons, the only real light came from the spotlight perched about the giant gold statue of Champion. Slick gave it little mind. He had a certain respectful abhorrence for the superhero, and he felt little need to stare into the face of Main City’s main guy. At least that’s what a fair number of citizens called him, though Slick never understood why.

Even if he wanted to spend time in silent adoration of the statuesque beauty of

Champion’s face, Slick didn’t have the time. Up on the second floor, two voices engaged in loud discussion as their footsteps grew closer to the stairs. Slick ducked behind the gargantuan base of the statue and made himself as small as possible. It could only be the guards, but why they shouted their conversation was beyond Slick. Granted, they probably spoke at normal, human volumes and it just sounded like a shout in the dead silence of the museum, but that was neither here nor there.

“I’m telling you, something's gonna happen here.” Slick wasn’t sure who it was, but he sounded worried. “That’s why I reached out to Champion. I mean, I know paintings aren’t a huge concern in the grand scheme of things, but,” the second voice interrupted the first.

“No, nonsense. Champion understood your concerns, and both of us feel it’s important to protect the culture of this city as much as its citizens. He would have come himself, but Nevermoore’s been unusually active lately. Seems he has an agenda, and Champion’s trying to stop that before things get too bad. That’s why he called me.”

“And we are so grateful to hear that, Major Optimism. When we saw that drone yesterday, we got concerned. I’m sure something’s going down. It has to be!” The guard was emphatic about that, and if only he knew that just a few feet away sat one of the museum’s paintings in the process of being stolen, he’d feel quite vindicated in his suspicions.

Slick was intrigued to hear that Major Optimism was here though. Make no mistake, he was glad it wasn’t Champion. Major Optimism was no joke, but he lacked the superpowers of his pal Champion. Slick had a plan to deal with Champion if it ever became necessary, but he lacked the gear required. Major Optimism could be outmaneuvered tonight if necessary. Still, the superhero spent most of his time in Twin City. It wasn’t unusual to see him in Main City of course. After all, the two metropolises were only separated by a river, but Slick hadn’t anticipated it. How was he going to get past the duo on the second floor?

Part of that quandary settled itself. The guard and Major Optimism started down the stairs towards Slick’s floor. While that settled the issue of the second floor, it introduced the newer, more frightening problem of the duo’s proximity to Slick. If they paced around the statue, or even caught the reflection in the window from the right angle, Slick was toast.

“How do things look in the security room?” Major Optimism inquired.

“Good enough. Frank at the Peterson Center checked in a few minutes ago. Said everything looked fine. Guss in the Main Gallery is asleep, so we’ll have to go check that ourselves.” Major Optimism nodded in response. Slick wasn’t sure which he felt most keenly, the disappointment that Gabriel’s name was really Frank, or the adulation that he’d gotten Guss’ name right. It was a small matter in light of his potentially dire circumstances, but he had to at least acknowledge the feelings.

Major Optimism and the guard were stopped opposite the statue now, and Slick did not like this one bit. Somehow he needed to fix this situation. He already had to inch around the statue a bit to keep out of sight, and these two did not appear to be leaving any time soon.

Eventually they would see him. After an intense moment of brain wracking, Slick had an idea.

His tablet came out again, but this time he tried to connect back to the Peterson Center. Given his distance, he couldn’t access his override node, and so that plan was out. If he couldn’t trigger an alarm there, he’d have to cause a distraction in this building. Sure, it was a less preferable plan, but it was better than being caught. Perhaps in the west hall would be best. It was furthest from where his car was parked, after all. Now the only question was what to do? A wicked grin crossed his face and he knew he had the right idea.

After a quick moment to fish through some video files, Slick changed the loops he’d set up in the Main Gallery. In the west hall he set four different loops to four different cameras that all showed the same guard on patrol in different parts of the room. For the heck of it, he also threw in a video from the Peterson Center to liven up the evening. Now, all he had to do was wait.

Time ticked by as Major Optimism and the guard droned on about mundane security issues. As they talked the ever so slowly paced around the statue. Slick had to adjust with each step they took, and it was nerve wracking. Why hadn’t they noticed the cameras yet! He fumed silently for a few minutes until the answer came to him. Guss was at the monitors, and Guss was asleep.

A torrent of curses coursed through his mind as he slunk down lower against the statue and tore through online videos in search of something to awaken the aged guard. Should he go for a woman’s scream, or perhaps a child’s cry? There was that annoying video with that dancing guy. Slick had no idea what that “music” was, but it was viral just now and absolutely terrible. Speaking of terrible music, he could play some Justin Beiber. That would motivate Slick to see what caused that unbearable racket.

In the end he opted for a nuclear alarm, and threw it to one of the monitors in the security closet. Major Optimism inched closer, but Slick didn’t have time to move. The video was now on the screen, but there was no sound. That was easy enough to fix. A stereo sat behind Guss would do to solve that problem, but only if it had bluetooth connectivity. Sure enough, it did. Slick loved the world he was born into. Everything could be connected to wirelessly.

The sound of nuclear sirens blasted through the security closet as Major Optimism was right on top of Slick. One more step and Slick would be in full view.

“Oh my goodness!” Guss’ ancient voice crackled over the radios.

“What is it, Guss?” Major Optimism’s companion asked into his own headset. The superhero stopped and turned to look at the guard with a raised brow.

“Mah screens are crazy, Jackie boy. I see Bill on four of ‘em, all in different quadrants. I’m seein’ the Peterson Center where the Jackson wall should be, and I have a siren goin off in here on the center monitor.” Guss was frenzied as he related the issues to Jack.

“Slow down, Guss, what are you saying?” Jack replied.

“They got us, Jack. We’re hacked.” Guss sounded so defeated, it almost broke Slick’s

heart.

“What is it?” Major Optimism asked.

“Cameras are crazy. We have some loops established and a couple of them are going haywire.”

“Where?”

“Don’t know,” Jack shrugged. “Where are they looped, Guss?” he spoke into his headset.

“Paddock Wing,” the old guard hollered back into the radio. That was it. Slick knew it had a name, but had referred to it as the west wing because he couldn’t for the life of him remember what it was.

Jack relayed the information to Major Optimism then awaited the hero’s assessment. “If they’re showing so much activity in the Paddock Wing, that can’t be their objective. Have someone do a sweep, but let’s head to the Grant Wing and check there. My gut says the cameras are just a misdirection.”

“I agree, Major Optimism. I’ll dispatch Ivan to the Paddock and have everyone else sweep the Grant.” Jack began yelling orders into his headset as the two dashed off in the opposite direction from Slick. He hadn’t thought that his distraction would prove too obvious, but since it got Major Optimism and Jack on one side of the building, it was fine for Slick’s purposes.

Granted, it was the wrong side of the building, but he could work with that.

Slick let the guards get to their destinations before he himself took off towards the front door. It wouldn’t be long until he was home free. Just in case though, he took out his

Neutralization Glove and primed it. One could never be too careful. He dashed up to the second floor and, with no resistance met charged up to the first. So great was his hurry that Slick almost missed the pair of guards in deep discussion at the main entrance.

Our villainous hero ducked behind a statue and listened in on their conversation. He gleaned little, but did gather that they were there to secure the entrance and ensure that no one got in or out. Slick cursed under his breath. Of course the escape couldn’t be easy.

“There’s a painting missing! I repeat there is a painting missing in the Peterson!” Slick jumped at the exclamation over the headset. The two guards at the door exchanged surprised looks as they continued to discuss the night’s events in ever more shocked tones.

While they prattled on like fools, Slick prowled through his mental diagram of the museum. Oh what he wouldn’t give for a super power about now. He could try a window, but they all opened to the front lawn, where there was no cover from the guards at the front door. The roof was an option, but not a good one. With the height added as an issue, he still had the same problem as the windows. Any path across the lawn would be clearly visible. He only had one chance at this point, and that was the front door. All he had to do was take care of Gloop and Grump. He was running out of names that began with G.

Slick slid away into a side avenue of the top floor. There weren’t many spaces not visible from the front entrance and the open spaces down to the second and third floor, so it took some doing to find a nice alcove he could utilize. Found it he did, and thankfully there was a truly gut wrenching, vomit inducing piece of “modern art” that he could vandalize to facilitate his escape.

Without guilt or hesitation, he shoved the “sculpture” over. Slick had no idea what it was supposed to be. The base was poured concrete, rough and slopped, while a metal pole stuck out the top. Spindly metal arms hovered on the poled, each laden with pots and pans, spoons and kettles. Supposedly it was a statement about feminism in the modern world. It sounded atrocious as it hit the ground.

“What the heck was that?” one of the door guards called. Slick smirked. “I’ll go check it out. Wait here.”

The guard, was it Grump or Gloop? Anyways, we’ll call him Gloop came over carefully, his hand on his firearm. Does museum security get firearms? One second, let me look at it a bit closer. Um… no no, that’s a taser. Still a threat, of course, but not as bad as a pistol. So, Gloop comes over with his hand on his taser, but Slick tucked away behind the wall. As Gloop rounded the corner, he saw the collapsed piece of art and inched forward carefully.

“What the heck happened to his hideo-er-er-er,” Gloop spluttered as Slick punched him in the side with his Neutralization Glove. The guard collapsed and twitched as electricity coursed through his body then dissipated, leaving him unconscious and mostly unharmed. One down, and only one more guard to go until freedom. Slick was getting to field test so many gadgets tonight. He’d have to do this more often.

Grump heard the slight commotion Gloop had made, but he wasn’t sure if his help was required. “Gloop?” he called. “Gloop, everything okay?” To be honest, he didn’t call his fellow guard Gloop, but I can’t exactly make out what he’s saying, so we’ll go with it.

Slick sprang from his hiding spot and sped through the shadows at the side of the gallery. Grump heard the sound cand called into his radio. “Activity at the entrance! Repeat, activity at the entrance!” The guard only had a moment to react as Slick rushed to close the gap. “Who are yo--” he began, but then he too fell victim to the mighty Neutralization Glove. Despite his triumph, Slick couldn’t hesitate to celebrate. Grump called for help, and it would be here. He burst into a sprint through the front door of the museum and down the steps, his cape flowing in his wake. Once he cleared the steps, Slick charged across the lawn in a straight line towards his car. It was parked only a few hundred yards past the light, and given his physical condition, Slick was sure he could make it there before anyone from the museum could stop him. If only they were the only ones Slick had to contend with.

A man plummeted out of the sky and slammed into the ground a couple of hundred feet

in front of Slick. Our villain slid to a stop and pulled his cape over his face as the new arrival clapped his hands and sent a telekinetic shockwave rippling over the lawn. Slick hunkered down as the wave washed over him and dissipated some way back. He’d known what to do. The scarlet and gold of Teledine. He was one of the more obnoxious and visible heroes that operated in the vicinity of Main City. With the gift of flight and telekinetic control, he wasn’t a pushover that was for sure.

“I believe you’ve got something that doesn’t belong to you,” he called across the lawn. Really? He went with that? Slick was not impressed. He did hear a sound behind him that spurred some entertaining ideas, however.

Without a reply Slick charged towards Teledine, his cape still pulled over his face to shield his identity. The hero seemed amused by this course of action and hunkered down to await Slick’s approach, his own red cape heroically a flow behind him. Slick might be able to beat Teledine in a straight fight. The hero had an impressive set of powers, to be true, but he was not renowned for his martial prowess. If Slick could get close enough, he’d probably win. It wasn’t a sure gamble, so Slick was glad his plan didn’t involve a fight.

When he had closed the gap to barely twenty feet, Slick dove to his right and rolled away from Teledine. He heard a crack from his back, and Slick had to worry that he’d damaged the painting with his maneuver. There wasn’t time to worry about that, as Slick watched the rest of his plan work beautifully.

He thought he’d heard Major Optimism’s footsteps on the grass behind him. The hero was close indeed, and collided with Teledine in a nasty way that sent the two heroes hurtling to the ground. Slick didn’t wait to see what happened next, but scrambled to his feet and charged on towards the Delage.

“You nincompoop!” Teledine screamed at his heroic companion. Slick could hear the exchange, but felt no great desire to hang around long enough to see its outcome.

“What are you doing here, idiot!” Major Optimism hollered back. “I had this under control before you got here!” Slick could see his car now, and he didn’t let the lover’s spat behind him slow him any.

“Clearly,” Teledine sneered. “Stand back and let me show you how a real superhero handles things.” Slick did not miss the insulting stress on the word super, and he doubted Major Optimism did either. It mattered little, for Slick threw himself into his car and took only a moment to toss the painting in the passenger seat before he started the vehicle and tore down the street. Teledine flew along behind Slick while Major Optimism sprinted down the street in their wake. How the non-super-powered Major planned on keeping up was beyond Slick, but he didn’t let that stop him putting the pedal to the floor. Teledine would be a bit of a problem. Slick had to make sure to avoid the telekinetic powers. Thankfully, Teledine didn’t have a lot of finite on-the-go control of his powers. If Slick could just keep moving, the superhero should have difficulty getting a grip on the moving vehicle.

As Slick dodged about the street, Teledine grew more openly agitated. Unable to stop the car, he resorted to throwing trash cans and moving dumpsters. He even flipped a car in Slick’s path, a decidedly unheroic sort of act if Slick were to be honest. Throughout all of this,

Slick kept ahead of his pursuer. In a last ditch effort, Teledine sent a shockwave down the street. Without a clear way to dodge it, Slick just swerved into a side street and let the wave of energy sail on down the street. Teledine overshot the turn, which bought Slick a few moments to plot his course. He couldn’t of course, because Major Optimism dropped onto the hood of the car. How did that man catch Slick so quickly? Clearly he knew some short cuts.

The next little while was spent in a desperate battle to dislodge the adamant superhero. Slick always had a bit of a soft spot for Major Optimism. Sure, Champion was a great hero and a fine man, but there was something about Major Optimism that spoke to his inherent goodness of heart. He never gave up, and he always did his best to do the right thing. Twin City couldn’t stop gushing about how much Major Optimism believed in the hearts of mankind. Simply put, he was just a good man, and Slick admired that. Oh, he was still going to hurl the guy from the hood of his car, but he would almost feel bad about it.

Despite his best efforts though, Major Optimism would not let go. He was latched onto the windshield wipers now, and Slick worried the things would fly off if he swerved much more. Those wipers were vintage. Major Optimism should know that. Slick plowed through some crates stacked in the alley, but even the collision couldn’t shake the hero. In the end, Slick took the turn back onto a main street a lot harder than he should have, but it almost worked to dislodge the Major. It was enough to give Slick some hope that he didn’t have a permanent fixture to his hood.

Teledine got involved again. Now that he had personal experience with the twit, Slick wondered how he got any public acclaim. The guy was a tool, and terrible at this whole heroing thing. Even now he used his powers to hurl a bicycle at Major Optimism. Slick assumed that was not the intended target, but that’s about all Teledine hit. With a certain sense of amusement, Slick watched the bicycle careen into a random passerby. The guy looked like a jerk though, so this was probably karma or something.

Of course the police were involved now, which always complicated things. Slick didn’t think they’d cause any real, lasting problems for him, but it was another obstacle to surpass before he could settle in at the lair with a good book or something. He wasn’t really sure what he’d do after this, and there was no time to think about it now. Two superheros swarmed around his car and he had one cop in front of him and the other behind. Things looked grim, but Slick had hope to see him through.

While he was thus engaged with his thoughts, Teledine got a hold of the front of the car with his telekinetic powers and began to lift. Slick’s back tires squealed in protest as the car slowed to a crawl. Teledine had an impressive grip on the vehicle, and Slick for the first time that night felt some genuine dread. Four police cars closed in while Major Optimism shuffled around to try and get a grip on the driver side door. With only a few more seconds left to think, Slick decided his best course of action was to trust his gut and slam on the breaks. It did little to the car’s speed, since he was practically stopped to begin with, but Slick did feel a jolt as Teledine readjusted his grip.

It looked like the flying hero felt the strain of his actions, so Slick decided to spice things up. He threw the car in reverse and hit the gas. The sudden shift in momentum was enough to break Teledine’s hold, and Slick’s front end slammed down as his car shot back past the cops that were tailing Slick. Due to the sudden change, Major Optimism slipped from the car and rolled off into the gutter. Slick pulled the car around in a beautiful move that left him faced away from all the police.

To his everlasting glee, Slick noticed Teledine hunched over on the street not very far at all to Slick. As the villain pushed the gas, he rolled down the window and primed his Neutralization Glove. Slick had never felt such sweet satisfaction as when he punched that obnoxious hero in the chin as he drove by. He savored the sight of Teledine spasming in the streets as Slick tore down the road and swerved into a side alley. He doubted he would forget that sight any time soon. In such a short amount of time, Slick had come to hate that man a great deal. Why, if he wasn’t already on a course of vengeance towards Greg, Slick might just divert his attentions to Teledine.

Now that the pursuit was largely behind him, Slick was able to make his way back to his warehouse with little incident. Before long he cruised down his personal subway tunnels and towards the warm comfort of his lair. Slick wasn’t one to get tired, not like this, but he felt as though he could sleep for a week. He would miss Fiona’s birthday, so that was out, but it was a nice thought nonetheless. At last he pulled into his parking space, shut down the car, and took the stairs back to his basement. He knew the garage would reorient the car in a moment, but Slick suspected it would need some work after that little stunt Teledine pulled. That was just one more reason to hate the hero. The repairs would have to wait, for Slick was tired. As it was he only just remembered to grab the painting from the passenger seat on his way up. Without any energy to deal with the stolen art tonight, Slick plopped the whole case on his workbench and shambled up to bed. He only paused once to shoot Reginald a thumbs up and a cooky grin.
